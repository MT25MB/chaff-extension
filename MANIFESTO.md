# The CHAFF Manifesto

## The Problem

Mass surveillance has become infrastructure.

Companies like Palantir Technologies have built systems that ingest data from hundreds of sources — social media posts, location history, license plate readers, Medicaid records, passport files — and use artificial intelligence to build comprehensive profiles of individuals. These systems are sold to immigration enforcement agencies, law enforcement, and intelligence services. They are used to identify, track, and deport people. They are used on US citizens. They are used without warrants.

The data that feeds these systems comes from the open web. From the things you post on Reddit. From the photos you upload to Instagram, which contain GPS coordinates of your home. From the behavioral fingerprint your browser leaves on every website you visit — a fingerprint so precise it can identify you even in incognito mode.

You never consented to this. The government did not subpoena your data — it bought it from data brokers who bought it from apps you never knew were watching.

## The Response

CHAFF is not a hack. It is not an attack. It is noise.

The word comes from the aluminum strips that World War II pilots dropped from aircraft to blind enemy radar systems. The radar still worked. The radar still swept the sky. But the sky was so full of false returns that no real aircraft could be distinguished from the noise.

That is what we are doing to the open web.

CHAFF generates synthetic human identities — fictional people who do not exist — and floods public platforms with their activity. These ghost identities are statistically indistinguishable from real humans. They post at human hours. They have consistent interests. They age over time. They contradict themselves across platforms exactly as real people do.

When surveillance systems ingest this data, they cannot tell the real from the fictional. Their confidence scores drop. Their identity graphs become unreliable. The weapon — mass data aggregation — is turned against itself.

## What We Are Not

We are not hackers. We do not exploit vulnerabilities. We do not intercept communications. We do not break into systems.

We are not fraudsters. Synthetic identities are fiction, protected expression. They are not used to obtain financial benefit or access restricted systems. They exist solely to generate noise.

We are not vigilantes. We operate in daylight, under our real organizational name, with published legal analysis, with academic framing, with the support of civil liberties organizations.

We are researchers, engineers, and citizens who believe that the right to privacy is not negotiable.

## The Legal Basis

Creating fictional personas is protected expression under the First Amendment (McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission, 1995). The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act does not cover Terms of Service violations against publicly accessible platforms (hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn, 9th Circuit 2022). Platform ToS violations are civil matters — platforms may ban accounts. They cannot prosecute users.

We operate as a nonprofit incorporated in Iceland, a jurisdiction with strong privacy protections and no data-sharing agreements with US law enforcement for this category of activity.

## Who This Is For

This is for the immigrant who doesn't know their social media is being monitored by ICE.
This is for the journalist whose sources could be identified by cross-referencing their digital footprint.
This is for the activist whose protest attendance is logged by license plate readers.
This is for the ordinary person who never agreed to have their Medicaid records used to build a deportation case.
This is for all of us, because mass surveillance does not stay targeted forever.

## The Goal

We do not want to exist forever. We want to exist long enough that Congress passes a federal data privacy law. We want to exist long enough that the legal and political foundation of mass commercial surveillance collapses under the weight of its own unreliability. We want to exist long enough that Palantir has to explain in a federal courtroom why its confidence scores should be trusted when the open web is demonstrably flooded with synthetic noise.

The best outcome is a world in which CHAFF is unnecessary.

Until then: fill the sky.
